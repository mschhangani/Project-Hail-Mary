Chapter 01 · τ = 0

Departure

The mission objective is simple to state and absurd to attempt: deliver a science payload to Tau Ceti, a sun-like star 11.9 light-years away, within a human lifetime. Chemical rockets would need over 100,000 years. The Crossing's spin-drive holds a steady 1.5 g instead — and lets Einstein do the rest.

drag to rotate · dots are facts

Mission objectives

① Reach Tau Ceti in under 4 years of crew time. ② Survive the interstellar medium at relativistic speed. ③ Decelerate — arriving at 0.995 c is not arriving, it's a very expensive flash. ④ Keep three humans alive, mostly asleep, the whole way.

As you scroll, Earth's gravity well falls away behind you. Watch the HUD: every value it shows is computed from the actual relativistic rocket equations, not animation curves.

Science insight

A light-year is a distance, not a time — about 9.46 trillion km, the distance light covers in one year. Saying Tau Ceti is "11.9 light-years away" means even a radio message takes 11.9 years to get there, one way, no exceptions.

Chapter 02 · the long burn

Building velocity

There is no air in space, so there is also no drag. By inertia, a coasting ship keeps its speed forever for free — which is exactly why the Crossing never stops thrusting: every additional second of burn is speed it gets to keep. A constant 1.5 g also doubles as artificial gravity, with "down" pointing toward the engines.

Notice the velocity gauge slowing down even though thrust never changes. At everyday speeds, 1.5 g adds about 14.7 m/s every second, like clockwork. Near light speed the same push buys less and less velocity — but keeps buying momentum and energy without limit. The speed of light isn't a wall you hit; it's an asymptote you chase.

Science insight

Why nothing with mass reaches c: kinetic energy grows as (γ−1)mc², and γ blows up toward infinity as v → c. Each extra 0.001 c costs more than the last; the final step would cost infinite energy. Light manages it only because photons have no rest mass.

The view ahead — relativistic aberration & Doppler shift

At high γ, starlight crowds toward your direction of travel and blueshifts — the sky bunches into a bright tunnel ahead and dims behind. This is the same slider, rendered honestly.

Science insight

Proper time (τ) is the time measured by a clock that travels with you — heartbeats, wristwatches, cell division. Earth time and ship time disagree, yet neither is "wrong": each clock is correct in its own frame. The traveler genuinely ages 3.9 years on a trip Earth measures at 13.1.

Chapter 04 · the silence

Calling home

Radio is light. Nothing — including mission control's reply — can outrun it. Choose how far out the ship is, transmit, and watch the round trip. The animation is sped up roughly two billion times; the counted delay is real.

one-way delay2.00 yr
round trip4.00 yr

A few months out, conversation is already impossible — by mid-voyage, a single question and answer spans a decade of Earth time. Crews of interstellar ships aren't remote workers; they are, functionally, alone. Mission planning must assume zero real-time help: every failure mode solved in advance or solved on board.

Science insight

Relativistic Doppler makes it worse: at 0.995 c receding, every transmission from Earth arrives stretched ~20× in frequency and duration — a one-minute voice message smeared across twenty minutes, redshifted deep out of band. Antenna pointing at interstellar range is its own nightmare.

Chapter 05 · the not-quite-vacuum

The cosmic environment

Interstellar space holds roughly one hydrogen atom per cubic centimetre, plus sparse dust grains, galactic cosmic rays, and the occasional micrometeoroid. Harmless — until you hit it at 0.995 c. At that speed the medium stops being emptiness and becomes weather.

impact energy, 1 mg grain @ 0.995 c ≈ 0.83 GJ — about 200 kg of TNT

Dust & micrometeoroids

Kinetic energy is (γ−1)mc². At γ ≈ 10, each milligram carries the punch of a small bomb. The fix is geometry: a thick ablative Whipple shield flown ahead of the ship, plus a slender profile that minimises frontal area.

Hydrogen becomes radiation

At relativistic speed, the thin hydrogen gas itself arrives as a beam of ~GeV protons — the ship is effectively flying into its own particle accelerator. Forward shielding mass and standoff distance are non-negotiable.

Cosmic rays

Galactic cosmic rays strike from all directions and shower through hulls. Best current answers: metres of water, propellant, or polyethylene around crew spaces — and keeping mission time short. Time dilation, for once, helps the crew here.

Science insight

Why braking matters as much as burning: all that lethal kinetic energy is yours too. A flyby at 0.995 c crosses the entire Tau Ceti system in hours. The Crossing spends fully half its mission — and half its propellant — flipped around, killing the speed it spent two years building.

Chapter 07 · the long sleep

Hibernation

In Project Hail Mary, the crew crosses in medically induced comas, tended by automated systems. The appeal is real engineering, not just drama: a sleeping crew consumes a fraction of the food, water, oxygen — and sanity — of a waking one, and dramatically shrinks the life-support mass the engines must push.

During suspension the simulation slows everything biological: a resting heart rate of ~60 bpm drops toward a torpor-like handful of beats per minute, core temperature falls, and metabolic demand collapses. Automated systems handle nutrient lines, muscle stimulation, and the thousand tiny corrections a body in stasis needs.

Watch the pod's vitals trace — it's running at the slowed rate, paced to the mission's gamma for a little poetic license.

Established science
  • Therapeutic hypothermia is routine medicine: cooling patients a few degrees for hours-to-days reduces metabolic demand and protects the brain after cardiac arrest.
  • Animal torpor is real and inducible. Ground squirrels drop to near-freezing body temperatures for months; researchers have triggered torpor-like states in mice by stimulating specific neurons.
  • Short-term human stasis research exists, including trials of emergency preservation (rapid cooling) for trauma surgery measured in tens of minutes.
Speculative / fictional
  • Months-to-years human hibernation has never been done; bone loss, muscle wasting, immune suppression and bedsores are unsolved at that scale.
  • Cryonic freezing and revival remains fiction — ice crystals destroy cells, and no frozen mammal has ever been revived.
  • Fully autonomous medical care of an unconscious crew for years, with no ground support, is far beyond current systems.

Chapter 08 · finding the way

Navigation

There is no GPS between stars, but there is something better: the stars themselves. The ship fixes its position by triangulating against pulsars — neutron stars whose radio ticks are as regular as atomic clocks — and checks its course by the slowly shifting parallax of nearby stars against the frozen background of distant ones. Science insight

Aiming at where the star will be: Tau Ceti moves through the galaxy at tens of km/s relative to the Sun. Over a 13-year crossing it drifts billions of kilometres, so the trajectory targets its future position — navigation between stars is a permanent exercise in leading the target.
